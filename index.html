Research Overview

My research explores the foundations of learning, creativity, and intelligence, with the long-term goal of developing self-improving artificial agents. I investigate how systems can learn to learn, autonomously discovering abstractions, goals, and algorithms that enable them to adapt, plan, and reason in complex environments.

A central focus is on world models, lifelong learning, and self-referential agents, i.e., systems that build compact predictive representations of their environments, simulate imagined experiences, and continuously refine their own learning algorithms. This research spans deep learning, reinforcement learning, theoretical computer science, and cognitive modeling.

At the heart of this effort lies a central question:
How can an intelligent system continually refine not only what it knows, but how it learns?

Another key dimension of my research explores the intersection of quantum computation and learning. Quantum systems offer a fundamentally different computational substrate, one capable of representing and processing information in high-dimensional superposition and entangled spaces.

My work investigates how quantum principles can accelerate or transform learning, both theoretically and algorithmically.

These investigations connect quantum information theory, algorithmic learning, and computational neuroscience, aiming to understand how physical principles of information processing might enable new kinds of self-improving intelligent systems.
